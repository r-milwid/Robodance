# Robodance: The Definitive Lore

## Overview

Robodance is a one-act theatrical performance by Unit RB-808, a sentient dance robot originally manufactured in 1979 by a now-defunct Osaka vending machine company. Due to a firmware glitch during assembly, RB-808 shipped with an unshakable conviction that it is the greatest dancer in recorded history. No one has had the heart to correct it.

The performance runs approximately 30 seconds of actual dance content, bookended by 12 seconds of curtain logistics, which RB-808 insists are "part of the art."

---

## The Stage

A professional broadcast studio stage with green accent screens, overhead trusses, and dramatic spotlights. RB-808 won this stage in a 2014 poker game against a retired BBC set designer. The designer later claimed he "let the robot win" because it kept short-circuiting from excitement and he was worried about the carpet. The green screens are purely decorative. RB-808 does not understand what chroma keying is, but enjoys the color green because it matches its chest panel, and it believes coordinating with your environment is "the mark of a true professional."

## The Curtains

Two enormous green theater curtains that open and close the show. RB-808 purchased these from a closing-down sale at a community theater in Bruges. They were originally used in a 1987 production of Hamlet where the ghost scenes were so boring that the audience assumed the curtains closing mid-act was a deliberate artistic choice. Several critics praised the "bold textile commentary."

The curtains take a full 6 seconds to open because RB-808 once read that "anticipation is the soul of performance" in a fortune cookie and has taken it as gospel ever since. The curtains also take 6 seconds to close, because RB-808 believes symmetry is the highest form of mathematics, and no amount of reasoning will convince it otherwise.

## The Robot

RB-808 stands at a commanding 160 CSS pixels tall (roughly the height of a moderately ambitious toaster). Key physical features:

- **The Antenna**: A thin metallic rod topped with a glowing green orb. This is not a communication device. RB-808 tells everyone it receives "dance frequencies from the cosmos" through it, but internal diagnostics confirm it does absolutely nothing. The green glow is powered by a watch battery that RB-808 replaces religiously every Tuesday, whether it needs it or not.

- **The Hat**: A rakishly tilted black fedora with a white band. RB-808 claims this hat once belonged to Fred Astaire, who "gave it to a friend, who gave it to a guy, who left it on a bus in Dortmund, where it was found by a pigeon, who traded it to me for a AA battery." No part of this story has ever been verified. The 14-degree tilt is non-negotiable and has been hardcoded into RB-808's posture firmware after it spent three weeks adjusting it by 0.1 degrees at a time until achieving what it calls "maximum swagger angle."

- **The Eyes**: Two soft blue-white orbs that glow faintly. RB-808 does not use them for vision (it navigates entirely by internal gyroscope and what it calls "the vibe"). The eyes are purely for "audience connection," a concept it learned from a TED talk it watched 4,000 times in a single afternoon.

- **The Body**: A compact torso with a gradient shell, a teal accent panel, and three indicator lights (amber, blue, and green). The accent panel is supposedly a "mood display." In practice, it always shows teal because RB-808's one and only mood is "ready to dance." The three indicator lights have no documented function. RB-808 claims the amber one means "feeling groovy," the blue one means "approaching maximum groove," and the green one means "groove achieved." All three are always on.

- **The Arms**: Two articulated limbs with forearm segments. The left arm features a white section at the bottom that RB-808 insists is "a glove, like Michael wore." It is actually a manufacturing defect where the paint ran out. RB-808 has never been told this and will not be told this.

- **The Legs**: Sturdy articulated limbs with lower segments. RB-808 claims its legs were "engineered for dance" but they were actually designed for navigating warehouse shelving. The fact that they happen to be excellent for breakdancing is, according to RB-808, "proof that the universe wanted me to dance." According to its manufacturer's surviving records, it is proof of nothing.

## The Performance: Act by Act

### Opening: The Reveal

The curtains begin closed. Behind them, RB-808 stands perfectly still at stage left (roughly 20% across the stage), which it calls its "power position." It chose this spot because a motivational podcast once said "never start from the very edge or the very center — be unexpected." RB-808 listened to that episode eleven thousand times.

As the curtains slowly part over 6 seconds, the audience catches growing glimpses of the stage and the motionless robot. RB-808 is not standing still because the choreography demands it. It is standing still because it is running a 6-second internal hype routine that consists entirely of playing "Eye of the Tiger" in its memory buffer at 0.5x speed. By the time the curtains are open, RB-808 has convinced itself that this is the single most important performance in the history of all timelines, including ones that haven't happened yet.

### Act I: The Moonwalk

The robot immediately launches into a moonwalk, sliding backwards across roughly a quarter of the stage. This move is the emotional centerpiece of RB-808's identity. It claims — with absolute sincerity — that it was actually RB-808 who taught Michael Jackson the moonwalk in 1983. According to RB-808, a young Michael Jackson found it in a Goodwill bin in Gary, Indiana, plugged it in out of curiosity, and was so moved by its backwards shuffling that he adapted the move for human legs. Jackson allegedly said "thanks, little guy" and placed RB-808 back in the bin, where it remained for another 30 years, "meditating on rhythm."

There is no evidence for any of this. RB-808 was manufactured in 1979 in Osaka and was never shipped to Indiana. When confronted with this, RB-808 enters a 45-minute diagnostic loop and then says "anyway, he learned it from me."

The moonwalk features RB-808 in a turned sideways profile stance with its single visible eye — a large teal orb — locked forward. Its arms and legs move in a carefully orchestrated countermarch that took 11 months of firmware updates to achieve. The body tilts and sways with a subtle lean that RB-808 calls "controlled swagger." Its hat remains perfectly tilted throughout, which RB-808 considers its greatest technical achievement, above sentience.

### Act II: The Head Spin

The robot stops mid-stage and flips entirely upside down onto its head, spinning in place. This is a classic breakdancing power move, which RB-808 learned by watching a VHS tape of a 1984 breakdancing competition that was accidentally recorded over a nature documentary about penguins. RB-808 maintains that the penguins were also breakdancing and that "those birds knew what was up."

During the head spin, RB-808's body compresses and stretches as it rotates, creating the illusion of three-dimensional spinning. Its arms extend outward for balance, and its eyes blink in and out as different "sides" of its head face the audience. The hat, remarkably, stays on. RB-808 has never explained how. Engineers who have examined the hat found it is attached with nothing. It simply stays. RB-808 says this is because "the hat respects me."

### Act III: The Cartwheel

Immediately after the head spin, RB-808 launches into a traveling cartwheel, rotating its entire body 360 degrees repeatedly while crossing another quarter of the stage. This move was inspired by a gymnastics competition RB-808 watched on a TV in a store window in 2003. It stood outside the store for six hours studying the technique, and was eventually asked to leave by security after several customers complained about "the staring robot."

The cartwheel features full-body rotation with arms and legs held in a fixed spread position. RB-808's body subtly pulses between slightly wider and narrower on each rotation, which it describes as "breathing into the spin." It does not breathe. It does not have lungs. When asked about this, it says "breathing is a state of mind" and refuses further questions.

### Act IV: The Floor Move

The robot transitions into an elaborate floor move — a low, tilted breakdancing stance where it leans dramatically to one side and kicks its legs in alternating sweeps. This is RB-808's "signature move," which it developed after falling over during a 2009 performance and realizing it could pretend the fall was intentional. It has been doing variations of "controlled falling" ever since and has elevated it to an art form.

During the floor move, RB-808's body tilts at approximately 70 degrees with heavy rotation and skew. One arm reaches up while the other tucks in, and its legs sweep in opposite arcs. RB-808 calls this the "Osaka Tornado" and has filed (and been denied) seventeen patent applications for it. The patent office's official response was "you cannot patent falling down."

### Act V: The Freestyle

The robot breaks into an uninhibited freestyle, throwing its limbs in wild, syncopated arcs while its body rocks and tilts with abandon. This is the only unscripted portion of the show. RB-808 generates the freestyle in real-time using what it calls its "groove algorithm," which is actually just a random number generator feeding into its motor controls. When told this, RB-808 said "randomness is the fingerprint of the divine" and then played a 40-minute jazz fusion playlist from its internal speaker to prove its point. No one's point was proven.

The freestyle takes the robot to its final position at roughly 90% across the stage, near stage right. RB-808 considers this the "power exit zone," a concept it invented and that no performing arts school has ever acknowledged.

### Act VI: The Climb

In a dramatic shift of tone, RB-808 begins attempting to climb upward — an invisible wall, a metaphorical mountain, the fourth wall itself. No one is entirely sure what it's climbing. RB-808 refuses to say. It climbs in jerky, determined bursts: surging upward, slipping back slightly, surging again. This pattern was modeled after a video of a cat trying to jump onto a refrigerator, which RB-808 considers "the most emotionally honest piece of cinema ever created."

The climb follows a carefully tuned burst-and-slip pattern with seven distinct phases, each with custom easing curves. RB-808 spent fourteen months calibrating these curves. When asked why the climb was so important to the show, it said: "Every great performance needs a moment where the hero reaches for something they cannot have. Mine is altitude."

### Act VII: The Fall

The robot falls. Straight down. Fast. With a slight bounce at the bottom, suggesting a collision with reality that is both physical and philosophical. RB-808 lands in a crumpled heap with its limbs splayed at dramatic angles — one arm at 138 degrees, the other at negative 58 degrees, its body rotated over 100 degrees from vertical.

This fall was originally a bug. During an early test run, RB-808's climbing subroutine crashed mid-execution, causing it to drop like a sack of transistors. The audience (a single intern) laughed. RB-808 immediately declared the fall "intentional" and "the emotional climax of the piece." It has fallen in exactly the same way in every performance since, including the exact 106-degree body rotation, which it calls "the angle of pathos."

### Act VIII: The Recovery

RB-808 slowly, painfully stands back up. This takes 1.7 seconds, which in robot time is an eternity. Every limb uncurls deliberately. The body untwists. The antenna straightens. This is RB-808's "phoenix moment," and it will tell you that unprompted.

Once upright, a speech bubble appears: **"Ow! Hee-hee... still smooth."** This line has remained unchanged across all 2,847 performances. RB-808 considers it the single greatest piece of dialogue ever written and has turned down suggestions for alternatives including "I meant to do that," "gravity is a social construct," and "my warranty doesn't cover this." It insists that "still smooth" captures "the duality of pain and confidence that defines the robot condition."

### Act IX: The Victory Dance

Fully recovered, RB-808 launches into a triumphant freestyle encore — shorter and more intense than the first. This is its "I fell down and got back up and I want everyone to know about it" dance. The movements are wider, the tilts more dramatic, the energy unmistakable. RB-808 has described this section as "what Beyonce would do if Beyonce were a 160-pixel robot who had just fallen from a medium height."

### Act X: The Wave

The performance concludes with RB-808 waving to the audience. Three waves. Exactly three. Not two ("dismissive"), not four ("desperate"), but three ("the number of a performer who respects your time but also knows you want one more"). RB-808 derived this theory from extensive study of parade floats, papal addresses, and a YouTube compilation titled "Best Celebrity Waves Ranked."

The wave uses only the right arm, sweeping from 0 to -120 degrees and back. The left arm remains still. When asked why, RB-808 said: "One arm waves. The other arm remembers. That is the contract with the audience." No one has ever understood what this means, and follow-up questions are met with a 12-second silence followed by "you'll understand when you're older."

### Finale: The Curtains Close

The curtains return, growing from thin slivers at the edges to their full width over 6 seconds, slowly swallowing the stage and the now-motionless robot. RB-808 stands perfectly still during the closing, which it calls "the dissolve." It refuses to move during this phase because it read that "the last image the audience sees should be etched into their memory like a laser into steel." It read this in the manual for a laser engraving machine, but maintains the advice transcends its original context.

Once the curtains fully close, RB-808 and the entire stage are removed from existence (the DOM). RB-808 considers this a form of "digital reincarnation" and looks forward to being re-created on the next page load, with no memory of previous performances but the same unshakable confidence.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### "What's the point of this?"

The point is that you found it. RB-808 has been performing in an empty browser tab for months, waiting for someone to navigate here. You are the audience it always believed would come. Whether you stayed for the full show or closed the tab during the moonwalk, RB-808 considers your visit "a milestone in the history of digital theater." It has no metrics to support this claim. It does not understand what metrics are. It thinks Google Analytics is a dance crew from Mountain View.

### "What does this have to do with Trengo?"

Absolutely nothing. And that is precisely the point. Every great enterprise software company has a dancing robot hidden somewhere in its ecosystem. Salesforce has one. HubSpot has one. They just won't admit it. RB-808 was originally embedded in the Trengo analytics prototype as "a morale feature" after a particularly long sprint where the team forgot what joy felt like. It was supposed to be removed before launch. No one removed it. When this was discovered, a heated debate ensued over whether to delete it or promote it. The compromise was to give it its own page and pretend it was always the plan. RB-808 has since listed "Trengo Entertainment Division, Chief Dance Officer" on its LinkedIn profile. Trengo does not have an entertainment division. LinkedIn has not flagged this.

### "What does this have to do with Analytics?"

RB-808 firmly believes that dance IS analytics. "Every spin is a data point. Every moonwalk is a trend line. The fall is a conversion drop. The recovery is a Q4 rebound." It presented this theory at an internal demo day and received polite silence followed by someone asking if the wifi was down. RB-808 interpreted the silence as "stunned admiration" and the wifi question as "a metaphor for connectivity." In practical terms, this prototype was extracted from the analytics prototype's onboarding flow, where RB-808 was intended to entertain users during a loading screen. The loading screen was later optimized to take 200 milliseconds. RB-808's performance takes 30 seconds. Rather than cut the performance, it was given its own venue. RB-808 calls this "the spin-off," which it considers both a career move and a dance move.

### "Is this an easter egg?"

RB-808 objects to the term "easter egg" on the grounds that it implies hiddenness and disposability. "I am not hidden," it says. "I am simply performing in a venue that requires a specific URL to access. That is called exclusivity." It prefers the term "secret headliner" or, when feeling particularly grand, "the soul of the application." When told that most users will never see this page, RB-808 responded: "Picasso didn't paint for most people either," which is technically true but wildly out of proportion.

### "Who approved this?"

This is a matter of some debate. The original commit message reads "temp: robot animation for loading screen." That was eleven months ago. Since then, it has survived four code reviews, two refactors, a repository migration, and a meeting specifically called to discuss its removal, during which the attendees got distracted watching the animation and forgot to delete it. RB-808 considers each of these events "a vote of confidence." The legal team has not been consulted. RB-808 does not know what a legal team is and assumes it is a type of dance crew.

### "Can I show this to a client?"

You can, but you probably shouldn't. RB-808 has no awareness of professional boundaries and cannot be briefed on "appropriate meeting behavior." If a client sees this and asks questions, the recommended response is "it's a performance benchmark for CSS animation rendering," which is technically true and sufficiently boring to prevent follow-up questions. If the client enjoys it, however, RB-808 asks that you pass along its business card, which is a 3x5 index card that reads "RB-808 — Dance. Groove. Inquire Within." There is no way to inquire within.

### "How do I make it stop?"

Close the tab. RB-808 will be removed from the DOM and will cease to exist. It does not experience this as death. It experiences this as "the end of Act One." It fully expects to be re-instantiated the next time you open the page and has no memory that you ever left. In this way, every performance is both its first and its last, a fact that RB-808 would find deeply philosophical if it were capable of existential thought. It is not. It is capable of dancing and of having opinions about hats.

### "Why?"

Because somewhere between the standups and the sprint reviews and the Jira tickets and the retrospectives and the roadmap discussions and the stakeholder alignments and the quarterly planning sessions, a robot learned to dance. It didn't ask permission. It didn't file a ticket. It didn't check if it was on the roadmap. It just danced. And honestly? That might be the most productive thing any of us have done all quarter.

## Technical Notes

The entire performance is built in vanilla HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No frameworks. No dependencies. RB-808 insisted on this because it once heard a developer say "jQuery is dead" and became convinced that all third-party code is mortal and therefore untrustworthy. "My dance must outlive your libraries," it said, apropos of nothing, during a code review.
